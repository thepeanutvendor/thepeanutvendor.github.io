A data-based chess learning experiment

24 March 2026·936 words·5 mins
Author
Ghassan Shahzad

The Mission Statement
#

I’ve been quite obsessed with chess recently. Over the next , I plan to use science to improve my game. Chess is an abstract strategy game, and mathematics is quite a useful tool in analysing it. For this reason, chess players have access to a number of metrics (accuracy, game and tactics elo, blunder/win rate, etc) that can inform their study, and that we can use to analyse the effects of various chess learning (and actual) ideas (see below for some examples). Few players do this in-depth like I plan to, probably for good reason: the simple formulae for improving at chess usually work without obsessing over stats. Nor is this going to be a study - it would be a faulty one with n=1. Moreover, there are time constraints involved here: I don’t want to spend too long on any one idea, because I am a human with a short life-span and I’d like more to enjoy chess than to make it boring. In sum, this is just a fun experiment. I do intend to be rigorous, but keep these constraints in mind.

I know next to nothing about chess statistics and programming; I will make an effort to learn the former, and maybe the latter.

The structure of each post here will be simple. I will take an hypothesis, like these ones:

  • Does listening to music while playing worsen or improve play? Does genre matter?
  • Quantity or quality in games (blitz, or rapid?)
  • There’s a lot of ways to practice tactics: by difficulty, motif, and so on. What’s the best way, if any?
  • Should you practice tactics before or after games?

I will test it through one to two weeks of play, holding all other variables (type of game, tactics, analysis, etc) constant. Note that the number of confounding variables out of my control are numerous and most are out of my control, another thing to keep in mind. Once done, I will look at various metrics, some general (like rating), others tailored to the specific hypothesis, to see if the hypothesis holds true.

The First Experiment
#

So what will my first experiment be? Nothing radical, actually. I want to set up a sort of baseline. This baseline will involve those basic habits that everyone recommends, the holy grail, tailored to my time constraints. Will this holy grail work? I’m already sort of adhering to these, but loosely. I’ll define it below:

  • 60 minute chess block, at least 5x a week
    • 20 minutes of tactics
    • 1-2 games of rapid (10+0, or 15+10, probably the latter)
    • Analysis (usually one notable game)

I have a habit of playing too many games. For now, I plan to eliminate that entirely and stick to two, maybe three max. For tactics, I do either ChessKing’s Chess Tactics for Beginnners by motif or ChessTempo standard and blitz; I’ll focus on the latter, and only do the former in addition. I will upload my analyses from here on publicly. I occasionally watch chess videos, but not in any significant way.

It’s worth asking if this is a baseline or my first experiment. I already have this plan noted out; the only difference may be order and duration; I do end up doing tactics daily, but may do them after games or for a shorter period of time. I’m going to bank on the guess that I’m already adhering to this schedule broadly, and doing it with more rigour won’t make much of a difference, and that the data for the next two weeks will thus constitute a proper baseline.

Now for the measurements. The most important one is actually simple: tracking adherence to this system. If I’m not abiding by these rules, testing them in a meaningfully way is impossible. Aside from that, since my hypothesis is quite simple, I will track simpler and fewer metrics. These will be:

  • Adherence rate
  • Rapid rating (on chess.com)
  • Tactics rating
    • ChessTempo standard
    • and ChessTempo Blitz
  • Win rate
  • Accuracy
  • Average opponent rating
  • Blunder rate
  • Flagging rate (amount of games I lose on time)

After this experiment, lasting maybe two weeks, I will get an idea of my direction in chess (whether I’m pleateauing, improving, etc), and a projection of where my metrics would go without any intervention. This will allow me to create and test other ideas with greater accuracy and isolation. The point is for these habits to always exist: if I carry on playing and practicing tactics randomly, it will make experiments quite tough; hence why I call this a sort of baseline.

Starting Statistics
#

My username on Chess.com is GShah12345. I have a rapid rating of 1233, which has jumped 81 points in the past week. My ratings on other time controls shouldn’t be taken too seriously for now - I mostly play them unseriously. My ChessTempo Standard rating is 1309.4, and Blitz rating is 1403.9. I’ve been inactive on both recently, so they should not be relied upon either. Every post, I will compile my various statistics and analyse them manually.

Community Participation
#

I’m thinking of getting Reddit (and other) chess communities, especially those of beginners like me, involved in this. This will help me increase my sample size, and help them compare and learn from these ideas too. I’d especially like to try out fun and unconventional experiments. A recent post I wrote on Reddit got quite a few positive responses, but I’ll likely need to put out a deliverable of my own before starting the first group experiment. Logistics seem difficult though, and it might be low-level. Still, an idea to consider!s
